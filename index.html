About Me

My name is Brian Lee and I am a 2023 alumnus graduated from Virginia Tech with a Bachelor's of Science in Computational Modeling and Data Analytics (CMDA) and minors in computer science and mathematics. My interests lie in the use of data science techniques in physical science problems, including bioinformatics and physics. In addition, I am interested in applying data science techniques to develop the understanding on machine learning methods to become safe, effective, and efficient tools of the future.

My experience consists of data visualization, data analysis of complex high dimensional datasets, and the design of software tools that aid the research process such as data pipelines or visualization modules. My coursework is diverse and interdisciplinary in computer science, mathematics, and statistics. My cirriculum favors courses focused on math modeling methods in dynamical systems, matrix theory, and modeling solutions to partial differential equations.

RESEARCH

Transfer Learning in GAN Training

Training effective generative adversarial networks (GANs) from scratch can often be a challenge. Not only is the training process costly in time and computational resources, but datasets of even slightly insufficient sizes encounter siginificant training failures.

This project evaluates both the efficacy and efficiency of transfer learning as a viable method of training generative adversarial networks (GANs) on limited datasets of images assembled from Instagram. A paper on my results submitted to the ITEA Journal of Test and Evaluation was approved for the June 2023 issue.

Combinatorial Coverage for Dataset Exploration Tool

When pursuiing model generalization, the role of combinatorial interactions between features present in a datset is often overlooked. Intuitively, a dataset that covers a larger portion of possible feature combinations trains a more robust model, as described by the combinatorial coverage metric.

This project aimed to create a dataset explorer program that integrates splitting of the dataset, model training and testing, and visualizes the relationship between combinatorial coverage and model performance.

PROJECTS

Comparing Latent Space Representations of Protein Sequenceing Models

To understand the special relationship between a protein's amino acid sequence and its function, bioengineers of today have turned to machine learning approaches to model it. While models exhibit strong predictive power, their weights and outputs lack any biological semantics being learned.

This CMDA capstone project was conducted under the sponsorship of the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) and completed by me and three other CMDA undergraduate teammates explores whether or not latent outputs of protein sequences learned by machine learning models are similar or different.

Game of Life using Message Passing Interface

Precise and large-scale simulations have their own trade-off with computational limits. Such programs can be processed in parallel, but just how much added benefit is gained by doing so?

In this project for Computer Science Foundations of CMDA (CMDA3634) at Virginia Tech, I implement and study the benefits of using parallel computing through scalability studies.

Alex Ovechkin Goal-Scoring Exploratory Data Analysis and Visualization

Washington Capitals elite Alexander Ovechkin has taken second place for all-time career goals scored in NHL history and shows no sign of slowing down. Although it is known he has a talent for scoring goals, game data suggests that he is in a league of his own entirely.

In one of my earliest personal projects, I use basic data preprocessing and statistical methods to extract characteristics and patterns in Ovechkin's goal-scoring. They are subsequently used in striking and creative visualizations to easily convey his unmatched proficency when compared to his peers leaguewide.
